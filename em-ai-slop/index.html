Maxwell's Equations: The Foundation

These four equations form the bedrock of classical electromagnetism, describing how electric (E) and magnetic (B) fields are generated and altered by charges and currents, and by each other.

Integral Form

The integral forms relate fields over surfaces and along loops to the sources within or enclosed.

1. Gauss's Law for E: Relates electric flux through a closed surface to the enclosed charge.

$$ \oint_S \mathbf{D} \cdot d\mathbf{A} = Q_{enc} = \int_V \rho \, dV $$

2. Gauss's Law for B: States that there are no magnetic monopoles; magnetic field lines always form closed loops.

$$ \oint_S \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{A} = 0 $$

3. Faraday's Law of Induction: A changing magnetic flux through a surface induces an electromotive force (voltage) around the boundary loop.

$$ \oint_C \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = -\frac{d}{dt} \int_S \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{A} $$

4. Ampère-Maxwell Law: Magnetic fields are generated by electric currents (\(I_{enc}\)) and by changing electric fields (displacement current).

$$ \oint_C \mathbf{H} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = I_{enc} + \int_S \frac{\partial \mathbf{D}}{\partial t} \cdot d\mathbf{A} = \int_S \mathbf{J} \cdot d\mathbf{A} + \int_S \frac{\partial \mathbf{D}}{\partial t} \cdot d\mathbf{A} $$

Here, \(\mathbf{D} = \varepsilon \mathbf{E}\) is the electric displacement field, \(\mathbf{H} = \frac{1}{\mu} \mathbf{B}\) is the auxiliary magnetic field, \(\rho\) is the volume charge density, \(\mathbf{J}\) is the current density, \(\varepsilon\) is permittivity, and \(\mu\) is permeability.

Differential Form

The differential forms describe the local behavior of the fields at any point in space and time.

1. Gauss's Law for E: The divergence of D is the local charge density.

$$ \nabla \cdot \mathbf{D} = \rho $$

2. Gauss's Law for B: The divergence of B is always zero.

$$ \nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = 0 $$

3. Faraday's Law: The curl of E is related to the time rate of change of B.

$$ \nabla \times \mathbf{E} = -\frac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t} $$

4. Ampère-Maxwell Law: The curl of H is related to the current density and the time rate of change of D.

$$ \nabla \times \mathbf{H} = \mathbf{J} + \frac{\partial \mathbf{D}}{\partial t} $$

The \(\nabla \cdot\) (divergence) operator measures how much a field spreads out from a point, while the \(\nabla \times\) (curl) operator measures how much it circulates around a point.

Bridging the Forms: Vector Calculus

How do we get from the integral to the differential forms? Two key theorems from vector calculus are the bridge:

  • Divergence Theorem (Gauss's Theorem): Relates the flux of a vector field through a closed surface to the divergence of the field within the volume enclosed by that surface.
    $$ \oint_S \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{A} = \int_V (\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F}) \, dV $$
  • Stokes' Theorem (Curl Theorem): Relates the circulation of a vector field around a closed loop to the flux of the curl of the field through any surface bounded by that loop.
    $$ \oint_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = \int_S (\nabla \times \mathbf{F}) \cdot d\mathbf{A} $$

Gauss's Law (E): Integral \(\rightarrow\) Differential

Step 1: Start with Integral Form & Charge Density
\( \oint_S \mathbf{D} \cdot d\mathbf{A} = Q_{enc} = \int_V \rho \, dV \)
Step 2: Apply Divergence Theorem (Left Side)
\( \int_V (\nabla \cdot \mathbf{D}) \, dV = \int_V \rho \, dV \)
Step 3: Argument for Arbitrary Volume
Since this must hold for *any* volume V, the integrands must be equal point-by-point.
\( \nabla \cdot \mathbf{D} = \rho \)

Faraday's Law: Integral \(\rightarrow\) Differential

Step 1: Start with Integral Form
\( \oint_C \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = -\frac{d}{dt} \int_S \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{A} \)
Assuming the loop C and surface S are fixed in space, the time derivative can move inside the integral:
\( \oint_C \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = -\int_S \frac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t} \cdot d\mathbf{A} \)
Step 2: Apply Stokes' Theorem (Left Side)
\( \int_S (\nabla \times \mathbf{E}) \cdot d\mathbf{A} = -\int_S \frac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t} \cdot d\mathbf{A} \)
Step 3: Argument for Arbitrary Surface
Since this must hold for *any* surface S bounded by C, the integrands must be equal point-by-point.
\( \nabla \times \mathbf{E} = -\frac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t} \)

Time-Harmonic Fields: The Complex Convention

Many electromagnetic phenomena involve fields oscillating sinusoidally in time (e.g., light, AC signals). Dealing with sines and cosines in differential equations can be cumbersome. Complex exponentials offer a powerful simplification.

We assume fields vary as \( e^{-i\omega t} \), where \(\omega = 2\pi f\) is the angular frequency. (Note: This is the common physics/engineering convention. Some texts use \(e^{+j\omega t}\), leading to sign differences in subsequent formulas, especially the imaginary part of \(ε_c\).)

Phasor Representation

A real, time-harmonic field like \( E_x(t) = E_0 \cos(\omega t + \phi) \) can be represented using a complex phasor. We write the full field as:

$$ \mathbf{E}(\mathbf{r}, t) = \text{Re}\{ \mathbf{\mathcal{E}}(\mathbf{r}) e^{-i\omega t} \} $$
$$ \mathbf{B}(\mathbf{r}, t) = \text{Re}\{ \mathbf{\mathcal{B}}(\mathbf{r}) e^{-i\omega t} \} $$

Where \(\mathbf{\mathcal{E}}(\mathbf{r})\) and \(\mathbf{\mathcal{B}}(\mathbf{r})\) are complex vector fields called phasors. They contain the spatial dependence, amplitude, and phase information. For example, \( \mathcal{E}_x(\mathbf{r}) = E_0(\mathbf{r}) e^{i\phi(\mathbf{r})} \).

The key advantage comes from the time derivative:

$$ \frac{\partial}{\partial t} \left( \text{Re}\{ \mathbf{\mathcal{E}}(\mathbf{r}) e^{-i\omega t} \} \right) = \text{Re}\{ \frac{\partial}{\partial t} (\mathbf{\mathcal{E}}(\mathbf{r}) e^{-i\omega t}) \} = \text{Re}\{ (-i\omega) \mathbf{\mathcal{E}}(\mathbf{r}) e^{-i\omega t} \} $$

Therefore, in the phasor domain, the time derivative operator \(\frac{\partial}{\partial t}\) is simply replaced by multiplication by \(-i\omega\).

Maxwell's Equations in Phasor Form (Differential)

Applying the \(\partial/\partial t \rightarrow -i\omega\) substitution to the differential forms (assuming source terms \(\rho\) and \(\mathbf{J}\) are also represented by phasors \(\rho_{\mathcal{P}}\) and \(\mathbf{\mathcal{J}}\) or are zero):

$$ \nabla \cdot \mathbf{\mathcal{D}} = \rho_{\mathcal{P}} $$
$$ \nabla \cdot \mathbf{\mathcal{B}} = 0 $$
$$ \nabla \times \mathbf{\mathcal{E}} = -(-i\omega \mathbf{\mathcal{B}}) = i\omega \mathbf{\mathcal{B}} $$
$$ \nabla \times \mathbf{\mathcal{H}} = \mathbf{\mathcal{J}} + (-i\omega \mathbf{\mathcal{D}}) = \mathbf{\mathcal{J}} - i\omega \mathbf{\mathcal{D}} $$

Notice how the time derivatives are gone, leaving spatial derivatives and algebraic terms – much simpler to solve!

Materials Matter: Constitutive Relations

Maxwell's equations involve four fields (\(\mathbf{E}, \mathbf{B}, \mathbf{D}, \mathbf{H}\)), but only two are independent. The material properties link them through constitutive relations. For linear, isotropic, homogeneous (LIH) media:

Permittivity: Relates electric displacement field D to electric field E.

$$ \mathbf{D} = \varepsilon \mathbf{E} $$

\(\varepsilon\) (F/m) describes how a material polarizes in response to E. Often written as \(\varepsilon = \varepsilon_r \varepsilon_0\), where \(\varepsilon_0\) is the vacuum permittivity and \(\varepsilon_r\) is the relative permittivity (dielectric constant).

Permeability: Relates magnetic field B to auxiliary magnetic field H.

$$ \mathbf{B} = \mu \mathbf{H} $$

\(\mu\) (H/m) describes how a material magnetizes in response to H. Often written as \(\mu = \mu_r \mu_0\), where \(\mu_0\) is the vacuum permeability and \(\mu_r\) is the relative permeability.

Conductivity (Ohm's Law): Relates conduction current density J to electric field E in conducting materials.

$$ \mathbf{J} = \sigma \mathbf{E} $$

\(\sigma\) (S/m) describes how easily charge carriers move through the material under the influence of E, leading to energy dissipation (heat).

These relations hold for both the real time-dependent fields and their complex phasors in LIH media:

$$ \mathbf{\mathcal{D}} = \varepsilon \mathbf{\mathcal{E}}, \quad \mathbf{\mathcal{B}} = \mu \mathbf{\mathcal{H}}, \quad \mathbf{\mathcal{J}} = \sigma \mathbf{\mathcal{E}} $$

Complex Permittivity: Real to Complex Fields (Bohren Ch 2.3 Logic)

Now, let's combine the phasor form of Maxwell's equations with the constitutive relations, focusing on how conductivity (loss) can be incorporated into a complex permittivity, simplifying the equations further.

Consider the Ampère-Maxwell Law in phasor form:

$$ \nabla \times \mathbf{\mathcal{H}} = \mathbf{\mathcal{J}} - i\omega \mathbf{\mathcal{D}} $$

Substitute the constitutive relations \(\mathbf{\mathcal{J}} = \sigma \mathbf{\mathcal{E}}\) and \(\mathbf{\mathcal{D}} = \varepsilon \mathbf{\mathcal{E}}\) (assuming \(\varepsilon\) is real for now):

$$ \nabla \times \mathbf{\mathcal{H}} = \sigma \mathbf{\mathcal{E}} - i\omega \varepsilon \mathbf{\mathcal{E}} $$

Factor out \(\mathbf{\mathcal{E}}\):

$$ \nabla \times \mathbf{\mathcal{H}} = (\sigma - i\omega \varepsilon) \mathbf{\mathcal{E}} $$

The goal is to make this equation look like the lossless case (\(\sigma = 0\)), which would be \(\nabla \times \mathbf{\mathcal{H}} = -i\omega \varepsilon \mathbf{\mathcal{E}}\). We can achieve this by defining an effective complex permittivity \(\varepsilon_c\) such that:

$$ \nabla \times \mathbf{\mathcal{H}} = -i\omega \varepsilon_c \mathbf{\mathcal{E}} $$

Comparing the two forms for \(\nabla \times \mathbf{\mathcal{H}}\), we must have:

$$ -i\omega \varepsilon_c = \sigma - i\omega \varepsilon $$

Solving for \(\varepsilon_c\), divide by \(-i\omega\):

$$ \varepsilon_c = \frac{\sigma}{-i\omega} + \frac{-i\omega \varepsilon}{-i\omega} = \frac{i\sigma}{\omega} + \varepsilon $$

Thus, the complex permittivity is defined as:

$$ \boxed{ \varepsilon_c = \varepsilon + i \frac{\sigma}{\omega} } $$

It has a real part and an imaginary part:

  • Real Part: \(\varepsilon' = \varepsilon\) (related to energy storage, polarization)
  • Imaginary Part: \(\varepsilon'' = \frac{\sigma}{\omega}\) (related to energy loss, conductivity)

(Note: With the \(e^{+j\omega t}\) convention, the imaginary part becomes \( - \sigma / \omega \)). Our \(e^{-i\omega t}\) convention leads to a positive imaginary part for loss.)

Using \(\varepsilon_c\), the Ampère-Maxwell law becomes elegantly simple again:

$$ \nabla \times \mathbf{\mathcal{H}} = -i\omega \varepsilon_c \mathbf{\mathcal{E}} $$

And if \(\rho_{\mathcal{P}} = 0\), Gauss's Law becomes:

$$ \nabla \cdot (\varepsilon_c \mathbf{\mathcal{E}}) = 0 $$

If \(\varepsilon_c\) is uniform in space, this simplifies further to \(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{\mathcal{E}} = 0\).

Python Example: Calculating Complex Permittivity

Here's how you might calculate \(\varepsilon_c\) in Python:


import numpy as np

# Constants
epsilon_0 = 8.854e-12  # Vacuum permittivity (F/m)

def calculate_complex_permittivity(epsilon_r, sigma, frequency):
    """
    Calculates complex permittivity using the e^-iwt convention.

    Args:
        epsilon_r (float): Relative permittivity (real part, unitless).
        sigma (float): Conductivity (S/m).
        frequency (float): Frequency (Hz).

    Returns:
        complex: Complex permittivity (epsilon_c) in F/m.
        float: Real part (epsilon') in F/m.
        float: Imaginary part (epsilon'') in F/m.
    """
    if frequency <= 0:
        raise ValueError("Frequency must be positive.")

    omega = 2 * np.pi * frequency
    epsilon_prime = epsilon_r * epsilon_0
    epsilon_double_prime = sigma / omega
    epsilon_c = epsilon_prime + 1j * epsilon_double_prime

    return epsilon_c, epsilon_prime, epsilon_double_prime

# Example Usage: Water at microwave frequency (approximate values)
freq = 2.45e9  # 2.45 GHz
eps_r_water = 78.0
sigma_water = 0.05  # S/m (can vary)

eps_c, eps_p, eps_dp = calculate_complex_permittivity(eps_r_water, sigma_water, freq)

print(f"Frequency: {freq / 1e9:.2f} GHz")
print(f"Relative Permittivity (real part input): {eps_r_water}")
print(f"Conductivity: {sigma_water} S/m")
print("-" * 20)
print(f"Complex Permittivity (epsilon_c): {eps_c:.4e} F/m")
print(f"   Real part (epsilon'):      {eps_p:.4e} F/m")
print(f"   Imaginary part (epsilon''): {eps_dp:.4e} F/m")
# Often expressed relative to epsilon_0:
print(f"Relative Complex Permittivity (epsilon_c / epsilon_0): {eps_c / epsilon_0:.2f}")

Summary & Quiz

We've journeyed from the fundamental integral and differential forms of Maxwell's equations, used vector calculus (Divergence and Stokes' Theorems) to connect them, simplified oscillating fields using the complex phasor notation (\(e^{-i\omega t}\)), incorporated material properties (\(\varepsilon, \mu, \sigma\)), and finally arrived at the concept of complex permittivity \(\varepsilon_c = \varepsilon + i\sigma/\omega\). This powerful tool allows us to treat wave propagation in lossy media using equations that retain the simple structure of the lossless case.

The imaginary part of \(\varepsilon_c\), \(\varepsilon'' = \sigma/\omega\), directly accounts for energy dissipation due to conductivity.

Glossary

Complex Permittivity (\(\varepsilon_c\))
An effective permittivity for time-harmonic fields that incorporates both the material's dielectric response (\(\varepsilon'\)) and its conductive losses (\(\varepsilon''\)). Defined as \(\varepsilon_c = \varepsilon' + i\varepsilon'' = \varepsilon + i\sigma/\omega\) for the \(e^{-i\omega t}\) convention.
Conductivity (\(\sigma\))
A material property (S/m) quantifying how easily electric current flows under an applied electric field. Related to energy dissipation.
Constitutive Relations
Equations linking auxiliary fields (\(\mathbf{D}, \mathbf{H}\)) to fundamental fields (\(\mathbf{E}, \mathbf{B}\)) and current density (\(\mathbf{J}\)) to \(\mathbf{E}\), characterizing the electromagnetic properties of a medium (e.g., \(\mathbf{D}=\varepsilon\mathbf{E}\), \(\mathbf{J}=\sigma\mathbf{E}\)).
Curl (\(\nabla \times\))
A vector operator measuring the microscopic circulation of a vector field.
Differential Form
Form of Maxwell's equations describing the local relationships between fields and sources at any point in space and time.
Divergence (\(\nabla \cdot\))
A scalar operator measuring the microscopic flux source or sink strength of a vector field.
Divergence Theorem
Relates the integral of a vector field's divergence over a volume to the flux of the field through the closed surface bounding the volume.
Integral Form
Form of Maxwell's equations relating field integrals over surfaces or loops to enclosed sources or rates of change of flux.
Permeability (\(\mu\))
A material property (H/m) quantifying the degree of magnetization in response to an applied magnetic field.
Permittivity (\(\varepsilon\))
A material property (F/m) quantifying the degree of polarization (electric dipole formation) in response to an applied electric field. Related to energy storage.
Phasor
A complex number (or vector) representing the amplitude and phase of a time-harmonic quantity. The time dependence \(e^{-i\omega t}\) (or \(e^{+i\omega t}\)) is implicitly understood.
Stokes' Theorem
Relates the integral of a vector field's curl over a surface to the line integral (circulation) of the field around the closed curve bounding the surface.
Time-Harmonic
Describes a quantity that varies sinusoidally with time at a single frequency.
